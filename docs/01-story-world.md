# 01. Story and World

## Story Direction

The story is intentionally silly, theatrical, and petty. No one is fighting over the fate of the universe. Everyone believes they are, but they are wrong.

The world treats nonsense with extreme seriousness. That contrast is the main comedic engine.

## Core Premise

The continent of Tallania is home to many competitive kingdoms, but two powers dominate public embarrassment:

- The Sunnybottom Empire
- The Mudsnout Horde

For years, both sides coexisted in loud disapproval. Then disaster struck at the annual `Festival of Height, Hats, and Soup`.

During the ceremonial judging of the `Most Impressive Decorative Tower`, Emperor Flambard the Radiantly Excessive accused Chief Grubnuk Mudsnout of "bringing a barn with windows and calling it architecture." Chief Grubnuk replied by calling the Emperor's tower "a spoon holder for rich cowards."

Things escalated when somebody kicked over the sacred soup ladder.

No one remembers who did it. Everyone claims it was the other side.

Within twenty minutes, both factions declared a forever war.

## The Real Stakes

The official reason for the war keeps changing depending on who is speaking.

Possible "reasons" mentioned by characters:

- rightful ownership of the Festival trophy
- revenge for the insult to ceremonial soup culture
- legal control over tower height standards
- the right to use the word "glorious" in royal announcements
- unpaid mustard taxes

The player gradually realizes that nobody agrees on why the war began, but everyone is far too proud to stop.

## World Tone

Tallania is not a realistic fantasy world. It is a cartoon-strategy setting where:

- military briefings include personal complaints
- propaganda posters are wildly petty
- elite units have ridiculous titles
- rival commanders argue during battles
- every faction thinks it is refined and the other side is uncivilized

## Faction 1: The Sunnybottom Empire

### Identity

The Sunnybottom Empire is convinced it is the pinnacle of culture, architecture, hygiene, and proper spoon storage.

They love:

- polished armor
- banners
- rules
- official certificates
- announcing things
- sunlight

They hate:

- mud
- shouting without permission
- unmeasured tower growth
- soup-related improvisation

### Leadership

#### Emperor Flambard the Radiantly Excessive

A glittering ruler with too many medals, too many mirrors, and absolutely no ability to let an insult go.

Traits:

- dramatic
- vain
- convinced he is inspirational
- constantly poses during military briefings

### Sunnybottom War Philosophy

"If a tower is worth building, it is worth overbuilding."

Their military style is disciplined and defensive, but their personality is self-important and flamboyant.

## Faction 2: The Mudsnout Horde

### Identity

The Mudsnout Horde is a coalition of swamp clans, tunnel crews, mushroom cooks, and loud enthusiasts who believe all good towers should be crooked, practical, and smell a little dangerous.

They love:

- smashing regulations
- improvised siege tools
- yelling as a team-building activity
- suspicious meat
- nighttime raids

They hate:

- decorative stairs
- polished stone
- unnecessary etiquette
- anyone who says "artisan broth"

### Leadership

#### Chief Grubnuk Mudsnout

A broad, muddy leader who values action, appetite, and revenge for all tower-based disrespect.

Traits:

- blunt
- proud
- chaotic
- weirdly creative with insults

### Mudsnout War Philosophy

"If it falls on the enemy, it counts as engineering."

Their military style is aggressive, scrappy, and gloriously messy.

## Shared Story Theme

The story theme is not tragedy. The story theme is `overreaction`.

Both factions turn a dumb argument into a massive military campaign because:

- they are too proud to apologize
- they keep inventing new reasons to stay angry
- their sub-commanders are also idiots
- winning now matters more than being correct

## Campaign Structure

The campaign follows the growth of the war from local tower grudge to continent-wide public nuisance.

### Act 1: The Soup Ladder Incident

The player learns the basics of war while the factions argue about what actually happened at the festival.

Key mood:

- tutorial comedy
- exaggerated outrage
- everyone is confidently misinformed

### Act 2: The Great Towering Escalation

Both factions begin building bigger and stupider siege towers. Rival commanders enter the conflict with personal grudges and signature gimmicks.

Key mood:

- bigger egos
- more ridiculous units
- increasingly embarrassing propaganda

### Act 3: The Official Committee Crisis

A third neutral committee attempts to stop the war by holding a peace conference. This fails immediately because both factions instead argue over seating arrangements, napkin folding, and whether gravy counts as a diplomatic fluid.

Key mood:

- peak parody bureaucracy
- pompous speeches
- total policy disaster

### Act 4: The Final Extremely Important Showdown

The campaign ends at the rebuilt Festival grounds where both sides attempt to prove their tower philosophy once and for all.

The final battle is treated like destiny, even though it is still fundamentally about ego, soup, and decorative architecture.

## Narrative Delivery Style

The story should be delivered through:

- short mission intros
- character portraits with punchy dialogue
- post-level victory banter
- propaganda posters on loading screens
- silly announcer text

## Writing Rules

- Keep scenes short.
- Every major character should have a comic flaw.
- Avoid emotional tragedy or grim war language.
- Treat tiny problems like giant historical scandals.
- Let units and items carry jokes in their names and descriptions.

## Example Dialogue

Emperor Flambard:
"No empire survives the public insult of a bent staircase."

Chief Grubnuk:
"That wasn't a staircase. That was a fancy shelf pretending to be brave."

Battle Announcer:
"History will remember this day. Mostly because everyone keeps filing complaints."

## Story Outcome

The ending should not fully solve the world's stupidity. Even after the final victory, the factions should still find a new reason to argue.

Possible ending gag:

The war ends, then instantly resumes because the winners misuse the official trophy as a soup bowl.
